If you want to slay the inflation monster and want your investments to work for you, then equity is your best bet.

However, Equity without Knowledge of Portfolio Management can be Disastrous.

It is easier said than done that investment in equity will help you to generate wealth. While we are very analytical in our approach when it comes to our professional life, we are very emotional and many a time illogical when it comes to investing. Therefore, an average investor earns lower returns than the market returns. According to a study done in the US market at the end of 2018, the average investor greatly underperformed the stock market. Over the last 30 years, the average investor saw a return of 3.66%, whereas the S&P 500 had an average return of 6.73%.

Why is it so? Studies show that emotions led to such underperformance. Stock tips from your neighbouring panwala or from your brother working with one of the world’s best brokerage firm or following random WhatsApp advisory groups lead to investment based on emotions which do not result in prudent long-term investing decisions.

Here are some of the common investing pitfalls of retail investors (that need attention as a best practice):

1) Too many stocks in the portfolio: There are many of us who are fascinated by the ‘guru’ investors who regularly feature in newspapers and appear in business channels. We are eager to follow the success of these investors in the investment world by imitating their portfolio. They keep on accumulating stocks purchased by these investment gurus. After a certain period, they have stocks in the portfolio that run into a number of pages.

After a few years, these investors begin wondering what went wrong when they find that their portfolio has underperformed in comparison with others.

A reason to have more stocks in the portfolio is because it gives the benefit of diversification. Nevertheless, it has been proved mathematically that as you move beyond 15 stocks, the benefit of diversification diminishes. Therefore, instead of loading your portfolio with all sorts of stocks, your portfolio should have 15- 20 stocks that should have a judicious allocation across industries. Otherwise, you risk missing out on significant market opportunities.

2) Averse to selling at a loss: Once you have understood the concept of diversification and how to manage your portfolio with a lower number of stocks, you start identifying those ‘junk’ stocks that need to be exited. The problem starts here. You are ‘married’ to these stocks, emotionally attached and find it very hard to sell these at a loss. You always believe that the tide will change and one day the stock will climb upwards again. Nevertheless, it rarely happens. There are stocks like Suzlon and Kingfisher that never recovered and ruined investors’ portfolio. You will have to understand that there is a reason why the stock went down and why it remains there.

By not exiting, you are losing the opportunity to make your money bounce back by selling the losing stock and entering a winning stock.

Hence, if mistakes are made, it is wise to accept the mistake and move forward instead of sticking to them.

3) Trying to time the market: Against the conventional wisdom of selling high and buying low, most of the investors invest when the market is at a higher level and sell when the market falls. They do this because they believe they can time the market. However, history shows that hardly anyone has done this consistently. Therefore, time in the market is important rather than timing the market. Empirical studies show that if you miss some of the best performing days in the market while trying to get your timing right, your long-term returns get diminished a lot. For example, if you miss the top five trading days in terms of gain, then your returns come down drastically as compared with the returns if you had remained invested.

Therefore, the best way to enter the market is in a staggered manner, instead of trying to wait for the market to fall. Our study shows that if you invest in a disciplined way at equal intervals without giving much attention to the market level, you will generate better returns. Even while selling, it is always better to exit in a staggered manner.

4) Do not have appropriate asset allocation: Your friend explained to you that time in the market is important than timing and you took this point home. However, your portfolio may still not be performing according to your expectation. This could be due to poor asset allocation. This is based on the principle “Do not put all your eggs in one basket”.

Many investors believe that stock selection is what creates superior returns in a portfolio. It can come as a surprise to them that around 90 per cent of the portfolio returns are attributable to asset allocation. Asset allocation is equivalent to making a pizza or any other dish of your choice. You may not like an individual component of pizza or the other dish, such as chilli or pepper, but with pizza it tastes good. So you may always think that large-cap stocks are boring and slow movers. However, during a market downturn, they give stability to your portfolio. The best example is the year 2019 when they generated positive returns, while the darlings of previous year’s mid-caps and small-caps were deep in the red.

Asset allocation involves dividing your investment portfolio among different asset classes, categories and industries. You can allocate your entire portfolio corpus to large-cap, small-cap and mid-cap stocks. You can also allocate your equity portfolio into different sectors such as IT, FMCG, banking, etc.

5) No portfolio rebalancing: Asset allocation is important for better investment experience; however, many investors stop there. Nonetheless, it can be further improved if you keep on rebalancing your portfolio as and when needed. Rebalancing the portfolio is the process of systematically bringing each of its weight to its allotted allocation (which can change over years depending upon investor’s risk profile). There is empirical evidence to support the performance advantage of rebalancing.

Conclusion: These are some of the basic investing rules that a common investor does not follow and hence the returns are usually below the market returns. It has been academically proven that a disciplined approach to investing delivers higher market returns. It is boring, but it works. If you do not have discipline, you probably should not be managing your investments. Apart from the demands of your occupation on your time and efforts, there may be other limitations that may be hampering you from following these simple investing rules.
